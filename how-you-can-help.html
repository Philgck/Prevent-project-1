How you can help

Everyone can help prevent radicalisation and extremism. Below are some warning signs and symbols to watch out for

Below is a non-exhaustive list of warning signs that someone might be at risk of further radicalisation

Changes in Behavior
  • Isolation: The individual becomes more withdrawn, isolating themselves from friends, family, and their community.
  • Increased secrecy: They start keeping their personal life, online activities, and social interactions more private, especially regarding their views or interests.
  • Mood changes: They show signs of agitation, anger, or frustration, especially when talking about certain topics such as politics, religion, or current events.
  • Extreme emotional responses: Expressing extreme anger or violent reactions to specific issues or events, such as perceived injustice or discrimination.
Adopting Extreme Ideologies
  • Adopting 'us vs. them' mentality: They become fixated on viewing certain groups or individuals as enemies or threats, often based on race, religion, or nationality.
  • Idealizing violence: They start to romanticize or justify the use of violence to solve political, religious, or social issues.
  • Expressing hatred or intolerance: Displaying a sudden or increased expression of hatred, intolerance, or bigotry toward certain groups (e.g., religious, racial, or political groups).
  • Increased interest in extremist views: They start showing a marked interest in extremist political ideologies, literature, or propaganda (online or offline).
Engagement with Extremist Groups or Influences
  • Engagement with known extremist networks: They may start associating with individuals or groups that have extremist or violent views.
  • Increased online activity: Excessive engagement with extremist content on social media or websites, or communicating with individuals who promote radical ideas.
  • Traveling to conflict zones: Expressing a desire to travel to conflict zones or areas known for terrorist activity, or showing an interest in joining militant groups.
  • New associations: Forming relationships with individuals or groups who hold radicalized or extremist beliefs.
Behavioral and Physical Changes
  • Changes in appearance: Dramatic changes in dress or appearance to reflect a more radicalized identity (e.g., adopting certain symbols or attire associated with extremist movements).
  • Changes in social media profiles: The person may start posting extremist messages, symbols, or endorsements of violent ideologies online.
  • Expressing extreme pride in violence: They may express pride in or admiration for violent acts committed by extremists or terrorist groups.
  • Increased interest in militant or terrorist groups: Obsessive attention to terrorist organizations, extremist propaganda, or messages calling for violence.
Increased Sense of Victimization
  • Feeling marginalized or persecuted: The individual may feel a deep sense of being oppressed or victimized by mainstream society, which they may attribute to their ethnicity, religion, or political beliefs.
  • Conspiracy theories: They might begin to adopt or strongly believe in conspiracy theories, particularly those related to global political or religious power dynamics.
Language and Rhetoric
  • Glorifying violence: They may begin using language that glorifies violence, martyrdom, or violent jihad (in cases of religious extremism), or refer to violence as a legitimate means of achieving political or social change.
  • Demonizing others: Using increasingly polarizing and derogatory language towards groups they disagree with (e.g., calling for the destruction or exclusion of certain groups).
  • Emphasizing purity: Expressing views that call for ‘purification’ of a religion, nation, or group, often by eliminating those who don’t conform to a narrow set of beliefs or practices.
Disengagement from Society
  • Avoidance of traditional or community institutions: They may reject long-held community values, schools, or social groups and move away from established institutions such as education, family life, or religious communities.
  • Refusal to engage in debates or discussions: Displaying a lack of interest or an unwillingness to engage in open, respectful conversations about social, political, or religious issues.
Support for Violent Extremism
  • Seeking to justify terrorism: Expressing support for acts of terrorism, justifying attacks or violence committed by groups like ISIS, al-Qaeda, or other extremist factions.
  • Plans or threats: They may start talking about plans for violent acts or threaten harm to others, either directly or indirectly.
  • Rejection of democracy and legal norms: A belief that democratic systems and laws are illegitimate, often with the suggestion that these structures must be overthrown.
Signs of Radicalization in Young People
  • Sudden change in friends or activities: Particularly in young people, a shift towards associating with new friends who hold extreme views, or engaging in activities that promote radicalization.
  • Withdrawal from education: A decrease in interest or disengagement from school or higher education, as education may be viewed as corrupt or irrelevant to their new beliefs.
  • Increased interest in political or religious extremism: A heightened interest in radical interpretations of religious or political ideologies, often to the exclusion of other perspectives.
Pressure to Conform or Push Others Toward Extremism
  • Pressure to recruit others: Attempting to recruit or convince others to adopt extremist beliefs or join radical groups.
  • Influencing others in close circles: Influencing or coercing family, friends, or peers to adopt extremist views or participate in extremist activities.
